A little bit of slope makes up for a lot of y-intercept

Why you should focus on growth.

If you focus on growth, you’ll end up at your definition of somewhere good regardless of where you stand now.

Slopes of lines is an extremely simple concept. This relationship between x and y is so obvious in Math that it is not paid the attention it deserves. But I think this mathematical truism reveals a pretty good guideline for life.

If you have two lines, the line with greater growth will for sure be above the other one (and gain a lot of advantage), regardless of the starting points.

The real insight comes from changing the perspective on what the x and y axes can means — like anything good (y) and time (x).

Pic

The starting red line, even with a lower Y-intercept, eventually crosses the blue line. Simply because it has a little bit more slope. In other words, growth.

Then if the Y-axis is something good, depending on your definition of something good, it’s more advantageous to pick the red trajectory over the blue trajectory. This concept can be extended to several things.

Take knowledge for example. Nowadays, people emphasize too much how much you currently know (y-intercept), and not the rate of new learning or initiative (slope). However, how fast one learns is more promising than how much one already knows. That guy who’s been coding for five years? If you’re dedicated to programming enough, it’s a likely possibility that you can catch up to, and even surpass him in a year’s time.

That goes to say that you shouldn’t be afraid of trying new things. Even if you’re bad at something, you can always catch up. The kid on the varsity soccer team? Start playing eight hours per day and he won’t be looking as impressive in a few months. It’s never too late for venturing onto new things; you shouldn’t forgo fields of expertise entirely just because there are other people that are much better at it than you.

The takeaway is that if you focus on growth, you’ll end up at your definition of somewhere good regardless of where you stand now.

Try new things, practice intensely what you want to be good at, and always remember that a little bit of slope makes up for a lot of y-intercept.

First Principles

To truly innovate, start from the fundamental truths.

Reason from first principles rather than by analogy. With analogy, we do things the way they have been done or that others are doing. With first principles, you boil things down to the most fundamental truths and reason up from there.

When you’re trying to create a new product or service, it’s critical to use this framework. For example, first principles gave Elon Musk a big edge when building electric batteries for Tesla and SolarCity and rockets for SpaceX. Consider building a rocket. Conventionally, people would search for how much a NASA rocket is worth and make an estimate for what it would take to build one. Instead, Musk’s team analyzed the necessary parts of a rocket, then researched the prices of the raw materials of parts firsthand. As a result, the SpaceX team was surprised to learn that they could build a rocket that cost around two percent of the typical price. And three times as efficient in flight.
